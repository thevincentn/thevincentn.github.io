More Distance, Less Force

Let's recall what we've learned about work, force and distance.

Levers take advantage of the relationship between force and distance to move heavy loads. For example, 100J of work is needed to lift a 10N object 10m into the air. But the same amount of work can also lift a 100N object 1m into the air.

W = F x d

It takes 500J of energy to move a 50N object 10 metres

A lever has three important parts: the fulcrum, the load arm, and the effort arm. Mechanical advantage (MA) is created in a lever by increasing the length of the effort arm / effort distance (ED) compared to the length of the load arm / resistance distance (RD). A longer effort arm lets you lift heavier loads with less force. You can explore this relationship in the activity below.

Move the sliders to learn more.

Key Idea

You can use a smaller force to move a heavy load, as long as you increase the distance over which you apply that force.

Types of Levers

There are three classes of levers. The placement of the fulcrum determines whether the lever is a class 1, 2 or 3.

Same Formulas

You can use the same formulas to calculate mechanical advantage for levers, regardless of the type of lever.

What Do You Think?

If you can apply 10 lbs of force, how much weight do you think you could lift with a lever that has an effort arm that is four times longer than the load arm?

[[N]]

[[N]]

[[Y]]

[[N]]

Check Answer

Correct Answer: 40 lbs

Since the effort arm is four times longer than the load arm, the lever gives you a mechanical advantage of 4. That means your 10 lbs of force can lift a 40 lb load (10 lbs × 4 = 40 lbs).

Calculating Mechanical Advantage

You can calculate the mechanical advantage of any simple machine using the resistance force (RF) and effort force (EF).

MA = RF ÷ EF

The mechanical advantage of a lever of a lever that can lift a 100lb load with 10lbs of effort is 10.

Problem 1: You apply 15 lbs of force to lift a 90 lb load. What is the mechanical advantage?

Open All Panels

Step 1: Identify

  • Need: the mechanical advantage
  • Know: load = 90 lbs, effort = 15 lbs

Step 2: Organize

Find the formula that relates to the resistance force (RF) and effort force (EF) to mechanical advantage (MA).

MA = RF ÷ EF

Step 3: Set Up & Calculate

Substitute the values:

MA = 90 ÷ 15 = 6

Step 4: Decide

Does the answer make sense?

Yes! If you can lift a 90 lb load with just 15 lbs of effort, the mechanical advantage is 6. ✅

Final Answer: The mechanical advantage is 6.

Using Lengths

For levers, mechanical advantage can also be calculated using the effort arm length / effort distance (ED) and the load arm length / resistance distance (RD).

MA = ED ÷ RD

The mechanical advantage of a lever of a 8ft effort arm and a 2ft load arm is 4.
