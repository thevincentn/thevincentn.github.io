Mechanical Advantage

Mechanical advantage (MA) describes how different tools and devices help skilled tradespeople apply a small effort force (EF) to produce a larger output force (OF). If a device lets you lift 100 lbs using only 25 lbs of force, it means the device has multiplied your force by a factor of 4. So, the mechanical advantage is 4. One way to calculate the mechanical advantage is by using the following formula:

MA = OF ÷ EF

The mechanical advantage of a device that can lift a 100lb load with 10lbs of effort is 10.

Force Multipliers

People have used tools that create mechanical advantage for thousands of years. The earliest devices used by people to create mechanical advantage are called simple machines. There are six types of classical simple machines. These machines form the foundation of nearly every mechanical system used in construction.

Lever

A lever is a rigid bar that pivots on a point (called a fulcrum) to move a load using less effort.

Used in: Crowbars, seesaws, pry bars, nail pullers

Wheel & Axle

This machine uses a larger wheel attached to a smaller axle. Turning one turns the other, reducing effort.

Used in: Wheelbarrows, door knobs, screwdrivers

Pulley

A pulley uses a wheel and a rope to lift loads by changing the direction of force or multiplying it.

Used in: Cranes, hoists, flagpoles

Inclined Plane

A sloped surface that allows you to raise objects by spreading the effort over a longer distance.

Used in: Ramps, loading docks, moving trucks

Wedge

A wedge is two inclined planes joined together. It's used to push things apart, split them or hold them in place.

Used in: Axes, chisels, shims

Screw

A screw is an inclined plane wrapped around a shaft. It converts turning force into a strong lifting or clamping force.

Used in: Vises, bottle caps, wood screws

Simple machines create mechanical advantage by:

  • Changing the direction of a force
  • Changing the distance over which a force is applied
  • Changing the speed at which the work is done

In the lever example above, the worker was able to lift a 100 lb load using only 25 lbs of effort because the lever spread the force over a longer distance. This allowed the worker to do the same amount of work needed to lift the 100 lb load, but with less effort at any moment.
