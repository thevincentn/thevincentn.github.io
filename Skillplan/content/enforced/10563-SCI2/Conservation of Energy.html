Work In = Work Out

The simple machines we highlighted in the last module help us lift heavy loads by multiplying a small effort force. But there’s a trade-off: If you use less force, you have to apply it over a longer distance. This trade-off is a result of the Law of Conservation of Energy, which states that energy cannot be created or destroyed, only transferred or converted.

Law of Conservation of Energy

Energy cannot be created or destroyed, only changed from one form to another or transferred from one object to another.

Since energy is conserved, the work we put in must equal the work we get out. Work is the product of force and distance. So, the effort we apply (EF) over a distance (ED) equals the resistance force (RF) moved over its distance (RD). The Moment of Force Equation combines these relationships between effort and resistance over different distances.

EF × ED = RF × RD

Applying a 10 lb effort force over 5ft is the same as moving a 50 lb object 1ft.

Note

The moment of force equation is also called the torque equation. Torque is a force that causes rotation around a pivot point, and many simple machines change the direction of the input force around a pivot point.

Correct Answer: True

The moment of force equation can be rearranged as follows:

EF × ED = RF × RD

ED = RF × RD / EF

ED / RD = RF / EF

Since MA = RF ÷ EF, we get:

MA = RF / EF = ED / RD

Rearranging the Equation

The moment of force equation can be rearranged in a variety of ways in order to calculate different things you need throughout this module and on the job.

A piece of lumber is used as a lever to lift a box. The resistance force of the box is 735 N. A fulcrum is placed under the bar 762 mm from the box. The effort force is exerted 1 981 mm from the fulcrum. Calculate the effort force to lift the box.

EF × ED = RF × RD

EF × 1,981 mm = 735 N × 762 mm

EF = 735 × 762 / 1,981

EF = 272.72 N

Formula to find Effort Force

EF = RF × RD / ED

A worker uses a ramp to push a heavy box upward. The resistance force of the box is 300 N. The ramp length is 5 meters and the height of the ramp is 1 meter. Calculate the effort distance needed to lift the box using the inclined plane.

EF × ED = RF × RD

EF × ED = 300 N × 1 m

ED = 300 × 1 / EF

ED = 300 N

Formula to find Effort Distance

ED = RF × RD / EF

A heavy duty mechanic applies 20 pounds of force to the handle of a 6-inch ratchet with a 1-inch socket. Calculate the force the socket exerts on a bolt.

The socket radius = diameter ÷ 2 = 1 ÷ 2 = 0.5 inches

EF × ED = RF × RD

20 lb × 6 inch = RF × 0.5 inch

RF = 20 × 6 / 0.5

RF = 240 lbs

Formula to find Resistance Force

RF = EF × ED / RD

A pulley system is used to lift a crate. The effort force applied is 150 lbs and the length of the rope pulled is 6 feet. The resistance force of the crate is 500 lbs. Calculate the resistance distance in the pulley system.

EF × ED = RF × RD

150 lb × 6 ft = 500 lb × RD

RD = 150 × 6 / 500

RD = 1.8 ft

Formula to find Resistance Distance

RD = EF × ED / RF

Different Units

Different simple machines use slightly different versions of the moment of force equation. For example, distance may mean radius, diameter or distance to the fulcrum. Force may mean revolutions per minute, newtons or pounds of pressure.
