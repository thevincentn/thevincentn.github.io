True or False

If you apply a force to an object but it doesn’t move, you’ve still done work.

False

"Work" has a very specific meaning in science. No movement means no work is done.

True or False

The higher you lift a load, the more potential energy it has.

True

Potential energy increases with height.

Keep reading to learn more about work and energy.

Work

In physics, work has a very specific meaning. It's not about how tired you are — it's about moving loads using force. We describe work with this formula:

Work = Force x distance

It takes 500J of energy to move a 50N load 10 metres

So, if you apply a force and something moves in the same direction, you’ve done work.

Example

If you push a wheelbarrow with 50N of force over a distance of 10m, how much work would you have done?

Show Answer

W = F × d

W = 50N × 10m

W = 500 joules (J)

Correct Answer: You would have done 500J of work.

Work Unit

Work is measured in Joules (J).

Energy

Energy is stored work. It has the same units as work, the Joule (J). There are different types of energy you use or see on the job site.

Click on the arrows to learn more.

Kinetic Energy

The energy in moving objects like falling tools, swinging loads or moving trucks.

Potential Energy

Stored energy in a suspended load or compressed spring.

Thermal Energy

Heat generated from grinding, welding or running equipment

Electrical Energy

Powers your tools, lights, equipment

Chemical Energy

Found in fuel, batteries, adhesives

When the load is on the ground, it has no potential energy. The crane transfers energy to do the work needed to lift it. As the load rises, it gradually gains potential energy.

Careful

If the hoist holding the load fails, that potential energy turns into kinetic energy. The higher the load, the more speed and kinetic energy it gains as it falls. The final impact could be extremely dangerous.

Power

Power is the rate of doing work or transferring energy.

Example

If two workers each lift the same load 10 metres high, but one does it in half the time, the faster worker used more power. The job took the same amount of work, but it was done faster.

If work is how much you do, power is how fast you do it.

Watts

Power is measured in watts (W). One watter is equal to one joule of work done every second. Larger tools and machines are often rated in kilowatts (kW).

Example

A circular saw uses about 1500 watts of power. How much work is it using every second?

Show Answer

Correct Answer: It’s using 1500 joules of energy every second it runs. That’s a lot more than a hand saw!
