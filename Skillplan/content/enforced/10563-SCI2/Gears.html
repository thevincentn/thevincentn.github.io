Introduction

A gear is a rotating mechanical component with evenly spaced teeth around its edge, designed to mesh with other gears to transmit rotational motion and torque. A system with two or more meshing gears is called a gear train. The smaller of the two gears is called the pinion and the larger one is simply called the gear.

The gear ratio compares the number of teeth between two gears, and describes the mechanical advantage. You can explore this relationship in the activity below.

Transferring Motion & Torque

When one gear moves another in a gear train, we say it drives the other one and the force applied is the effort. The gear that gets driven will always turn in the opposite direction of the driving gear and is the resistence. In a two-gear system, the driving gear will either increase force or speed, depending on whether the pinion or the larger gear is driving.

Pinion DrivesGear Drives

If the pinion drives the gear, the system increases torque while reducing speed, providing a mechanical advantage in force. When determining gear ratios for mechanical advantage with force, we use the moment of force equation.

EF × ED = RF × RD

Mechanical Advantage = Gear Ratio = ED / RD = RF / EF

Force = # of Teeth or Torque

Distance = Rotations Per Minute (RPM) or Diameter/Radius


If the larger gear drives the pinion, the system increases speed while reducing torque, providing a mechanical advantage in speed. When determining gear ratios for mechanical advantage with force, we use the same equations shown earlier.

EF × ED = RF × RD

Mechanical Advantage = Gear Ratio = ED / RD = RF / EF

Force = # of Teeth or Torque

Distance = Rotations Per Minute (RPM) or Diameter/Radius


The trade-off between speed and force is a fundamental principle in gear systems.

Problem 3
