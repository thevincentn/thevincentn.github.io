Introduction

Hydraulic systems use an incompressible fluid, usually oil, to transfer and multiply force. When you push on a small piston, pressure is created and passed through the fluid, pushing a larger piston upward. This happens because of Pascalâ€™s Principle.

Pascal's Principle

When pressure is applied to a confined fluid, it is transmitted equally in all directions.

In a simple hydraulic system, two pistons are connected by a liquid. The pressure stays the same, but the force changes depending on piston size. If the large piston is twice the area of the small one, it will lift twice the weight but the smaller piston must move twice as far. You can explore this relationship in the activity below.

Mechanical Advantage

You can calculate mechanical advantage with hydraulics using any of the following equations:

Mechanical Advantage = Resistence Force / Effort Force = Resistence Area / Effort Area

The Moment of Force Equation

Because of the law of conservation of energy, the work done on one piston equals the work done by the other. This lets us use the moment of force equation below.

This equation helps us solve hydraulic problems by showing how force and distance trade off to keep work balanced.
