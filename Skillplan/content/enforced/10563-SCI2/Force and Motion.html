True or False

Forces cause things to move and stop objects that are already in motion.

True

If something is sitting still, a force is needed to get it moving. If something is already moving, it takes a force to stop it, speed it up or change its direction.

True or False

To accelerate a heavier object at the same rate as a lighter one, you need to apply more force.

True

The force required to accelerate an object is directly related to its mass.

Motion Basics

Every mechanical machine or system creates, controls or uses motion. Motion is made up of four different components.

Click on each to learn more.

Distance Time Speed Acceleration

Distance is how far something travels. It's usually represented by the letter d.

If you roll a wheelbarrow 10 metres across a slab, the distance is 10 metres. It doesn’t matter how fast or slow — it’s just how far something moves from point A to point B.

Time is how long it takes to travel a certain distance. It’s usually represented by the letter s.

For example, it might take you 5 seconds to roll the wheelbarrow 10 metres.

Speed is how fast something moves — it’s the distance divided by time.

Speed = Distance ÷ Time

We usually use the letter v  for speed to avoid confusing it with s  for seconds.

v = d ÷ t

So if you move 10 metres in 5 seconds:

Speed = 10 ÷ 5 = 2 metres per second

Acceleration is how quickly speed changes.

If you’re pushing that wheelbarrow and begin to speed up, you're accelerating. Acceleration is the change in speed over time.

Acceleration = Change in Speed ÷ Time

Change in speed is calculated by subtracting the starting speed from the final speed.

Change in Speed = Final Speed - Starting Speed

So, acceleration (a ) can be calculated using the following formula:

a = vfinal - vstart / time

Because we're dividing speed (m/s) by time (s), the unit for acceleration is m/s2.


Did You Know?

Any object that falls on earth accelerates at 9.8m/s2.

Force

When we apply force to something, we cause its motion to change. The more force we apply, the more it accelerates — meaning it speeds up. But the force also depends on the object’s mass. This relationship is described by the formula:

Force = mass × acceleration

It takes 300N to accelerate a 100kg object 3m/s2.

This isn't a formula you’ll use every day, but it’s useful to understand. Essentially, the more massive something is the more force you need to change its motion. So, if you're pushing a heavy object quickly, it requires a lot of force.

Units of Force

The Newton (N) is the standard unit of force and is equal to 1kg × 9.8m/s2. In some calculations we may also use pounds (lbs) instead of Newtons.

Check Your Understanding

If a crane operator accidentally drops a 1500 kg load, what is the force of gravity acting on the load as it falls?

Show Answer

F = m × a

F = 1500kg × 9.8m/s2

F = 14700 N

Correct Answer: The force due to gravity on the falling load is 14,700 N. This is the same no matter how high the load is dropped from, because gravity always pulls with the same acceleration (9.8 m/s²).

Height Doesn't Change Force

It might seem like dropping a load from higher up increases the force on it, but it doesn’t. Gravitational force depends only on mass and gravity. What does increase with height is the object’s energy, which we’ll explore in a future lesson.

Why It Matters

The relationship between force, mass, and acceleration is crucial in the skilled trades — sometimes, it's even a matter of life and death.

Examples

Click on each to learn more.

A truck carrying 3 tons of timber going too fast down a logging trail.

If the truck loses control, a large amount of force is required to slow it down safely. If it collides with something, the impact force could be catastrophic.

A small piece of shrapnel getting caught in a sanding belt.

Sanding belts move quickly and apply significant force. If that force is transferred to a small, low-mass object like shrapnel, it can become a dangerous projectile.

Improve Safety, Prevent Accidents

Understanding how motion and force work helps you move more safely, operate more efficiently and prevent accidents on the job site.
