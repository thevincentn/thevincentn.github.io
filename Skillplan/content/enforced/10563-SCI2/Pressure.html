True or False

A small force can create a lot of pressure if it's applied over a small enough area.

True

Pressure is all about how much force is spread out. If the area is small, even a modest force can produce high pressure.

True or False

Air and liquids both respond to pressure in the same way.

False

They behave differently. Liquids can’t be compressed, so pressure travels instantly. Gases can compress, so their pressure depends on volume and temperature too.

Keep reading to learn more about pressure.

Force Over Area

Pressure is the amount of force applied over a specific area. The smaller the area you apply a force to, the more pressure there is. That’s why a sharp nail goes in easier than a flat one. The sharp point concentrates the force into a tiny area. Pressure is described by the following formula:

Pressure = Force ÷ Area

You produce 80 psi of pressure if you exert 160lbs of force over a 2inch2 area.

There are a few different ways to measure pressure. Which one you use depends on the system or tool you're working with.

Pressure & Matter

Pressure behaves differently depending on the properties of the matter that a force acts on. This includes the object's state of matter, its temperature and the volume it occupies.

Solids Liquids Gases

In solids, pressure is focused and directional. It might push or press against something else. Think of a bolt clamping two beams together.

In liquids, pressure is distributed evenly in all directions. That’s how hydraulics work—they apply force to fluid to move pistons and lift heavy loads.

Like liquids, pressure acts in all directions with liquids. But gases are compressible. That’s key in systems like air brakes or pneumatic tools.


What Do You Think?

Hydraulics work by applying force to a fluid to move pistons and lift heavy loads.

[[Y]]

[[N]]

Check Answer

Correct Answer: True

Hydraulic systems, like those in heavy equipment, use fluid pressure to lift and move parts like arms, buckets or blades. A small piston pushes fluid into a larger cylinder. The pressure stays the same, but the larger area creates a greater force. This is how a small input can move a very heavy load. Force equals pressure times area.


Temperature & Volume

Pressure also has an interesting relationship with temperature. In general if the volume stays the same:

  • If you increase temperature, so does the pressure.
  • If you decrease temperature, so does the pressure.

That's why you might notice the pressure in your tires build up in the summer. As the heat goes up, so does the pressure.

Job Site Applications

Pressure shows up everywhere in construction. Whether it's tires, compressed air systems, hydraulics or water systems, understanding pressure helps us work with these different systems and objects safely.

Pressure might seem invisible, but it’s doing a lot of work behind the scenes. If left unchecked and uncontrolled, there can be very severe consequences.


Potential Hazards

When working around pressurized systems, be cautious of overpressure. Overpressure happens when the pressure inside a system — like a tank, pipe, or cylinder — goes beyond what it’s designed to handle. In the trades, this is a serious safety concern. If pressure builds up too high, it can cause:

  • Bursting of containers or pipes
  • Seal or valve failures
  • Explosions or flying debris

Preventing Overpressure

It all comes down to staying within the limits and giving pressure a safe way out when things go wrong.

  • Use pressure relief valves or rupture discs
  • Don’t exceed rated PSI or bar limits
  • Regular inspections and maintenance
  • Know the safe operating ranges for tools and systems
