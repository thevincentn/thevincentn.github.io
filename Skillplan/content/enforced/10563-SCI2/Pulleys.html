Introduction

A pully is a simple machine that uses the advantages of wheels and axles to make lifting or moving loads easier. A pulley is made up of a wheel with a rope around it. There are four types of pulleys.

Click on each to learn more.

FixedMoveableCompoundBox & Tackle

A fixed pulley is attached to a stable structure and stays in place when used. It helps lift a load by changing the direction of your force. For example, pulling down on the rope lifts the object upward so you can incorporate your body weight into the effort. However, a fixed pulley does not reduce the amount of force required to move the load.

Fixed pulleys don't multiply the effort force so they do not create mechanical advantage.

A moveable pulley is attached to the load, so it moves with it. This setup reduces the effort needed to lift the load by sharing the weight between multiple rope segments.

A single moveable pulley provides a mechanical advantage of 2.

A compound pulley, is a system of multiple pulleys, both fixed and moveable, working together. When you combine or chain several pulleys together you amplify the mechanical advantage.

The more moveable pulleys in the system, the easier it is to lift a heavy load. The trade-off is that you need a fixed pulley for each moveable pulley and you increase distance for the rope to travel.

A block and tackle is one form of a compound pulley that is mounted on a single frame. These are the most common types of pulley systems found in the trades. Diagrams of block and tackle pulleys often appear in an expanded view so that people can analyze the fixed and moveable pulleys they are made up of.


When working with pulleys, there are a few components and terms that's helpful to be aware of.

Tension

Tension is the force transmitted through the rope or cable in a pulley system. In ideal systems with no friction or slack, the tension is the same throughout a continuous rope. Tension is key to understanding how effort is distributed across the system and how mechanical advantage is achieved.

Sheave

A sheave is the wheel / groove inside a pulley that the rope runs over. It rotates to reduce friction and guide the rope. Larger or smoother sheaves help the system work more efficiently by minimizing resistance.

Becket

A becket is the fixed point where the rope is tied or anchored on a pulley block. It doesn’t move with the rope. In mechanical advantage calculations, the rope attached at the becket typically does not count as a lifting segment.

Calculating Mechanical Advantage

Basic Pulley Systems

In most cases, it's straightforward to determine the mechanical advantage of a pulley system. You can often just count the number of rope segments that support the load. Take a look at the following examples.

Click on each to learn more.

1:12:13:14:1

A pulley system with a 1:1 mechanical advantage consists of a single fixed pulley with one rope segment supporting the load. The fixed pulley does not multiply the effort force so it has a mechanical advantage of 1.

A pulley system with a 2:1 mechanical advantage has exactly one moveable pulley in it with two rope segments supporting the load through the sheave. It may be supported by one fixed pulley or none at all.

The effort force comes through one side of the moveable pulley through the tension in the rope. This tension must be the same on either side so each side applies the same effort force to lift the load up. This means the moveable pulley doubles the effort force to lift the load with 1/2 the work. Below you'll see two ways to visualize a 2:1 pulley system.

A pulley system with a 3:1 mechanical advantage has exactly one moveable pulley in it with three rope segments supporting the load. Two of the rope segments travel through the sheave and 1 is connected to the moveable pulley through a becket. It is supported by 2 fixed pulleys.

Below you'll see two ways to visualize a 3:1 pulley system.

A pulley system with a 4:1 mechanical advantage has two moveable pulleys supporting the load through the tension in four rope segments. Two of the rope segments travel through the sheave of one moveable pulley and the other two segments travel through the sheave of the remaining moveable pulley.

Below you'll see two ways to visualize a 4:1 pulley system.

So to determine the mechanical advantage of the most common pulley systems, you can just count the number of rope segments supporting the load.

Complex Pulley Systems

Calculating mechanical advantage in complex pulley systems isn’t as straightforward as counting the number of rope segments. In the trades, you don’t need to calculate the mechanical advantage but you do need to recognize these systems. The key difference is that pulleys lift other pulleys as if they were loads. Here are some examples.

Stacking Moveable Pulleys Stacking Fixed Pulleys Combining Types

In this 8:1 system, moveable pulleys lift other moveable pulleys. Each pulley doubles the mechanical advantage, so with 3 pulleys, the advantage is 2 × 2 × 2 = 8. This setup provides a high mechanical advantage with fewer pulleys, but it may cause the load to tilt as it’s lifted.

In this 3:1 system, fixed pulleys lift other fixed pulleys. The effort applied to the first pulley must balance on the other side. As the effort passes through the becket, it doubles. By the time it reaches the last rope segment, the mechanical advantage is 3:1 because 1 + 2 = 3.

This 5:1 system combines different pulley types. The first segment forms the same 3:1 system in the previous tab. At the top, the effort force is multiplied by 4. As the force passes through the moveable pulley and the final fixed pulley, it is split and lifted with twice the effort. The mechanical advantage is 5:1 because 3 + 2 = 5.

Remember, you will only need to determine the mechanical advantage on pulley systems where you can just count the number of rope segments supporting a load.

How much effort force is required to lift a 180lb load in this pulley system?


Need: Effort force (EF)
Know: MA = 4, RF = 180lb

Locate the formula: EF = RF ÷ MA

RF = 180 lb

MA = 4

Step 1: Write the formula: EF = RF ÷ MA

Step 2: Substitute: EF = 180 ÷ 4

Step 3: EF = 45

The effort force is reasonable because it is smaller than the resistance force.
