True or False

You only need to think about force when something is already moving.

False

Force matters even before something moves. It’s what causes motion to begin in the first place. Without force, nothing would start moving at all.

True or False

If two forces are equal and opposite, nothing will move.

True

When forces are balanced, there’s no change in motion. This is what keeps a scaffold steady or a beam supported.

Compression (Pushing Force)

A force that squeezes or compacts a material.

You see it in:

  • Load-bearing walls
  • Concrete columns
  • Scaffolding posts
  • Hydraulic jacks

Example: When you stand on a scaffold plank, your body weight creates compression in the support bars below.

Tension (Pulling Force)

A force that stretches or pulls a material apart.

You see it in:

  • Ropes, chains, cables
  • Crane rigging
  • Rebar in concrete (under load)
  • Suspension bridges or ceiling hangers

Example: A tower crane lifting a steel beam puts tension on the cables — too much, and something could snap.

Gravity (Weight Force)

A natural downward force from Earth pulling on every object with mass.

You see it in:

  • Anything being lifted, supported, or moved
  • Fall protection systems
  • Hoists, jacks, pulleys

Example: Knowing the weight of materials helps you choose the right tool or lift system.

Friction (Resisting Force)

A force that opposes motion between surfaces in contact.

You see it in:

  • Sanding and grinding
  • Brakes on equipment
  • Sliding tools or materials on rough surfaces

Example: Friction is great when you need grip, but it’s also the reason tools overheat and wear out.

Shear (Cutting or Sliding Force)

Forces acting in opposite directions, causing materials to tear or slide apart.

You see it in:

  • Scissors and shears
  • Bolts and rivets under strain
  • Paneling, sheet metal, or drywall seams

Example: A bolt head snapping off under pressure? That’s shear force in action.

Torque (Twisting Force)

A rotational force that twists a material or structure.

You see it in:

  • Drill bits
  • Torque wrenches
  • Levers
  • Shafts and axles in rotating machines

Example: Overtightening a bolt can create too much torque — and damage the threads.

Interactions with Forces

Whether you're lifting, building, cutting or installing something, you're not just dealing with one force at a time. Forces often combine, balance, or push against each other all at once.

Click on each to learn more.

Combining Opposing Balancing

When forces are working together, we get more total force in the same direction.

Example

Imagine two crew members working together to carry a ladder. The crew member at the back pushes while the one in the front pulls. Their combined effort overcomes the ladder’s weight (gravity) making the lift safer and smoother.

Forces can push or pull in opposite directions. When forces oppose each other, the stronger one wins — or the object stays still if they’re balanced.

Example

Friction opposes motion. When you're sliding a heavy object up a ramp, the ramp pushes back. That’s friction resisting your applied force.

Sometimes, forces cancel each other out. This balance is what gives a structure stability.

Example

A load lifted in place because the tension in the rope exactly matches the force of gravity pulling it down.
