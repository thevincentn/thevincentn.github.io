True or False

Force is what makes things move and stop objects already in motion.

True

If something is sitting still, it takes a force to get it going. If something’s already moving? It takes a force to stop it, or speed it up, or even change its direction.

True or False

The heavier something is, the more force you need to make it speed up at the same rate as something lighter.

False

The force required to accelerate an object is directly proportional to its mass. So, to accelerate a heavier object at the same rate as a lighter object, you need to apply more force.

Motion Basics

Every mechanical machine or system creates, controls or uses motion. Motion is made up of 4 different components.

Click on each to learn more.

Distance Time Speed Acceleration

Distance is how far something travels. It's usually represented by the letter d.

If you roll a wheelbarrow 10 metres across a slab, your distance is 10 metres. It doesn’t matter how fast or slow — it’s just how far something moves from point A to point B.

Time is just how long it takes to go that distance. It's usually represented by the letter s.

For instance, it may take you 5 seconds to roll that wheelbarrow 10 metres.

Speed is how fast something moves — it’s the distance divided by time.

Speed = Distance ÷ Time

We usually assign speed the letter v  to avoid confusing it with s  for seconds. So the formula for speed is:

v = d ÷ t

So if you move 10 metres in 5 seconds:

Speed = 10 ÷ 5 = 2 metres per second

Acceleration is how quickly speed changes.

If you’re pushing that wheelbarrow and you pick up the pace, you’re going faster every second. That's acceleration. You can also think of acceleration as the change in speed over a given amount of time.

Acceleration = Change in Speed ÷ Time

Change in speed is usually calculated by subtracting the final speed by the starting speed.

Change in Speed = Final Speed - Starting Speed

So acceleration, a, can be calculated using the following formula:

a = vfinal - vstart / time

Force

Force is what makes motion happen. Without force, we don't get any action. Every mechanical system — simple or complex — is designed to do one of the following:

Click on each to learn more.

Create Force Multiply Force Control Force

Some systems exist purely to generate the force needed to make something move, lift, cut or rotate.

Examples
  • Electric Motors: Turn electrical energy into rotational force (torque) to drive drills, saws, conveyor belts, etc.
  • Hydraulic Pumps: Build fluid pressure to push pistons or move machinery (like lifts or excavators).
  • Engines: Burn fuel to create motion — driving compressors, generators, or vehicles.
  • Your Own Body: When you swing a hammer or lift drywall, your muscles are generating mechanical force.

These systems don’t create new force — they help you do more with less by redistributing or amplifying the force you already have.

Examples
  • Pulleys: Multiply lifting power by spreading force across multiple ropes.
  • Levers: Use a longer handle (like a crowbar or pipe wrench) to lift or turn with less effort.
  • Gears: Increase torque (turning force) by changing gear size or ratio.
  • Wedges & Inclined Planes: Let you apply force gradually over distance — like pushing a load up a ramp instead of lifting straight up.

Some systems are designed not to make things move — but to slow, stop, redirect, or control the force that’s already in motion.

Examples
  • Brakes: Convert moving energy into heat to stop wheels or machines.
  • Clutches: Connect or disconnect the transfer of force (as in gear shifts or power tools).
  • Shock Absorbers / Dampers: Absorb force gradually (suspension systems, heavy equipment booms).
  • Valves in Pneumatics/Hydraulics: Control how much force is transmitted and where it flows.
  • Torque Limiters / Safety Couplings: Stop systems from applying too much force and damaging equipment.

Force Changes Motion

When we apply force to something, we're causing its motion to change. The more force we apply, the more it accelerates — meaning its speed increases. But the force we apply also depends on the mass of the object. This relationship is described by the following formula:

Force = Mass × Acceleration

While this is not a formula we need to use very often, it's helpful to understand how this relationship works. Essentially, the more massive something is the more force we need to change its motion. If you’re trying to push a heavy object quickly, you need a lot of force.

Why It Matters

The relationship between force, mass and acceleration is very important for skilled tradespeople. In fact, it can be a matter of life and death.

Examples

Click on each to learn more.

A truck carrying 3 tons of timber going too quickly down a logging trail.

If this truck loses control, a lot of force will be required to slow it down to a safe speed. If this truck were to collide into anything, the collision force transfered on impact could be catastrophic.

A small piece of shrapnel getting caught on a sanding belt.

Sanding belts move fast and can apply a lot of force. If this force gets transfered onto a small piece of shrapnel with little mass, the object could become a very dangerous projectile.

Improve Safety, Prevent Accidents

Understanding how motion and force works helps you move safer, operate smarter and prevent accidents on the job site.
