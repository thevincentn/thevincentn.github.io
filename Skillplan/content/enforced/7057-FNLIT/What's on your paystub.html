Breaking Down a Paystub

To clear the air for Jeffrey, let's take a look at a typical paystub and break down its different components. After you've looked at the paycheck we'll ask you some questions, so review the paystub closely.

Click on the different parts of the paystub to learn more.

Paystub information

Each paystub you receive has information about the paystub itself. This includes

  • your employer's information,
  • the period for which you are being paid, and
  • when you should expect to receive the money.

It's important to save your paystubs to keep track of your income especially if you have multiple employers for tax purposes.

Employee information

This is your paystub so there'll be information about you.

Earnings

In this section, you'll see information about money that you earned by working. It's separated into:

  • Current - For money earned during the reporting period, the two week period you're being paid for.
  • YTD - Which stands for "Year to Date", for money earned from the start of year to the end of the current reporting period.

Regular Hours

In this row, you'll see the money you earned for your regular hours of work during the current period and the YTD. The "rate" you see is what was agreed upon during your contract negotiations.

Your agreed upon work hours vary from company to company. On a regular day you'll be asked to work between 7 to 8 hours. Lunch breaks are not typically included in this time and you won't be paid for them.

Overtime

Overtime, or extra hours of work, is paid at different rates, depending on the number of hours worked. It is always your regular hourly wage multiplied by 1.5 or 2.

Time-and-a-half

Time-and-a-half is your regular wage multiplied by 1.5. You get time-and-a-half for any time worked over eight hours in a day, up to 10-12 hours depending on your employer's agreement with your union. You will also get time-and-a-half if you work over 40 hours a week.

Double time

Double time is your regular wage multiplied by 2. Depending on your agreement, you may get double time if you work over the 10-12 hours in a day or if you work on a statutory holiday (stat day), which is a holiday recognized by the government where employees typically get time off.

Pay in Lieu

Many Canadian employers reward their employees with time off for vacation or on stat days. In the skilled construction trades, workers are instead paid out a percentage of their wage instead of getting this time off (in lieu of). This percentage is typically 6% for vacations and 6% for stat days.

If you don't get your stat days off, you may earn this 6% in lieu of in addition to the double time you would be earning for working the stat day.

Deductions

When you first start your apprenticeship, you may be really excited about making $XX.XX an hour but when you look at your paystub, things may not live up to your initial expectations. But where does this money go exactly?

Deductions are amounts taken off of your pay as required by law, your union agreements, and any benefits you are subscribed to. There are 3 categories of deductions:

  1. Tax Deductions
  2. Before Tax Deductions
  3. After Tax Deductions

Tax Deductions

Tax deductions are amounts taken off of your pay as required by law. There are 3 types of tax deductions:

  1. Income Tax includes both federal and provincial taxes used for things like health care, infrastructure, and other societal services.
  2. Canadian Pension Plan (CPP) is a contribution made to a retirement plan for all Canadian workers. Although you are not earning this money now, it will support you in the future when you retire. Note that CPP is often not enough for retirement and you'll still need to prepare yourself. We'll talk more about ways to prepare for retirement throughout this course.
  3. Employment Insurance (EI) is a contribution made to support Canadian workers if they lose their jobs at no fault of their own. You'll likely use this benefit throughout your apprenticeship and into your career. We'll talk more about how to access this benefit later this module.

Other Deductions

In addition to tax deductions there are "before tax deductions" and "after tax deductions."

Before Tax Deductions

Before tax deductions are deductions made that you do not need to pay tax on and are taken from your earnings before any tax is deducted.

This includes your union dues, which you pay to receive the benefits of union representation, for example:

  • negotiated pay raises
  • health and pension benefits
  • minimum standards for working conditions
  • a grievance procedure

After Tax Deductions

On the other hand, after tax deductions are deducted from your pay after the tax deductions are calculated.

We'll talk more about the benefits of these deductions in the next lesson.

Total Gross

Your Total Gross Income is how much you earned in total before any deductions are taken from your pay.

Taxable Gross

Not all of your gross income is taxed. This section summarizes the gross income that you'll be taxed on. It's typically your gross income with the "before tax deductions" taken off.

Total Taxes

This is just the total from the "taxes" section under "deductions."

Total Deductions

Your Total Deductions is the total deducted from your:

  • Before Tax Deductions;
  • Tax Deductions; and
  • After Tax Deductions.

Net Pay

Your Net Pay, or net income, is how much you "take home" after the deductions from your gross income. This is the money you get to spend!

Pay yourself first

Many tradespeople put their “Pay in Lieu of - Vacation” money aside so they can have an income stream while taking a vacation between construction projects. This is a concept called “paying yourself first.”
