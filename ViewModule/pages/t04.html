Legal Authorities

Learning Outcome(s):

  1. Discuss legislation relevant to forensic photography.
    1. Review legislation authorizing forensic photography.
    2. Describe characteristics required of digital images as outlined in the Canada Evidence Act (1985).

Before taking photographs of a person for a forensic purpose, the legal authority to do so must be considered. Patrol members with Vancouver Police Department (VPD) issued phones may take photographs of minor victim injuries and suspects or persons in custody when the purpose is identification or reference.

In the case of victim injury photographs, permission is obtained from the individual and therefore the authority to photograph is granted through consent. When the person being photographed is a suspect or individual in custody, specific authorities are granted through the Identification of Criminals Act (1985).

Identification of Criminals Act (1985)
Identification of Criminals Act (1985)

According to the Identification of Criminals Act (1985), the following persons may be photographed when the purpose of the photograph is their identification:

  1. any person who is in lawful custody charged with or convicted of
    1. an indictable offence, other than an offence that is designated as a contravention under the Contraventions Act in respect of which the Attorney General, within the meaning of that Act, has made an election under section 50 of that Act,
    2. an offence under the Security of Information Act, or
    3. an offence punishable on summary conviction if that offence may also be prosecuted as an indictable offence described in subparagraph (i);
  2. any person who has been apprehended under the Extradition Act;
  3. any person alleged to have committed an indictable offence, other than an offence that is designated as a contravention under the Contraventions Act in respect of which the Attorney General, within the meaning of that Act, has made an election under section 50 of that Act, who is required under subsection 500(3), 501(4) or 509(5) of the Criminal Code to appear for the purposes of this Act by an appearance notice, undertaking or summons; or
  4. any person who is in lawful custody pursuant to section 83.3 of the Criminal Code.

Identification of Criminals Act, s. 2 (1), 1985

Consent and Forensic Photography

Consent related to forensic photography is not always at straightforward as it seems. Any inspection by a member is considered to be a search if a person has a reasonable expectation of privacy (R v Cole, 2012). If you are in a dwelling with shared areas and items, you must be careful to identify the privacy holder(s) and obtain consent for photography. Consent can also be revoked at any time.

Remember the three zones of privacy: personal, spatial, and informational. Consider these when taking forensic photographs.

Plain View Doctrine

The plain view doctrine is a power to seize, not a power to search (R v Jones, 2011). When an officer finds evidence unexpectedly in the course of their duties, in circumstances where it is at once obvious and without positive action on the officer’s part to make it observable, they have the right to seize it. The officer does not have the authority to conduct a search for further evidence under this exception. This would include taking photographs of the areas surrounding the evidence without warrant or consent.
