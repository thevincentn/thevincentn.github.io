Minor Injury Photography: Best Practices

Learning Outcome(s):

  1. Evaluate injury photographs for admissibility and effectiveness.
  2. Acknowledge the importance of maintaining the dignity of a subject during injury photography.
  3. Explain best practices to maintain the dignity of a subject during injury photography.

Injury photographs are still forensic photographs and must generally meet the same requirements. These images must be relevant, well exposed, and in focus; they must provide an unbiased and accurate documentation of the injury. In addition, the subject of these photographs are people â€“ so there are additional guidelines and practices to follow.

Best Practice

If the subject states that they have an injury that you cannot see, you may still take a photo of the area. Document that the subject stated that there was pain or an injury in that area, and that no visible injury was seen. Impartiality is key.

Header Card

As with other forensic photography, a completed header card must be included and must be the first photograph.

Overall of Victim

Similar to the photography of a crime scene, an overall image must be taken to show the victim. This helps to identify the subject, as well as help the viewer to understand the location of the injury in later photographs.

The purpose of this photograph is to provide documentation of the face, for identification purposes. Note that the image is well exposed and focused; make sure that any lighting sources do not create shadows that could obscure facial features.

Midrange: Location of Injury

Like scene photography, forensic images of an injury require midrange photographs. These help the viewer understand location of injury on a subjectâ€™s body and in relation to each other in the case of multiple injuries.

Close Up of Injury

After the location of the injury is established via overall and midrange photographs, close up images are required. Ensure that the injury is well lit but not over exposed and fill the frame as much as possible. Keep in mind that distortion can happen in the Axon app if you stand too closely to the subject.

Close Up with Scale

The final image of this injury is the close up with the scale. The scale provides the viewer with information about the size and the colour of the injury, so it is important to ensure that the colour scale is not cut off in the photograph. Consider asking the subject, if they are able, to hold the scale so that you are able to stabilize the cell phone with both hands.

Always taking the required header card, overall, midrange, and close up photographs in a systematic fashion will ensure the relevancy of your images and become second nature with practice. Following a consistent practice with forensic photography will also allow you to speak confidently about it in court, if ever required.

Best Practice

If the injury is bandaged, do not remove the bandage for the photograph. If the subject wants to pull the bandage back to show the injury they may, but do not touch it yourself.
